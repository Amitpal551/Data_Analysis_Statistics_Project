import scipy.stats as stats

# Split data into male and female groups
male_spend = df2[df2["Gender"] == "Male"]["MonthlySpend"]
female_spend = df2[df2["Gender"] == "Female"]["MonthlySpend"]

# Independent t-test
t_stat, p_val = stats.ttest_ind(male_spend, female_spend, equal_var=False)  # Welch’s t-test

print("t-statistic:", t_stat)
print("p-value:", p_val)

# Interpretation
if p_val < 0.05:
    print("Result: Significant difference in spending between males and females.")
else:
    print("Result: No significant difference in spending between males and females.")

Choose test based on data-types¶

Male vs Female spend (Gender categorical, MonthlySpend numeric) → Independent t-test.

Education level vs MonthlySpend (Education categorical, MonthlySpend numeric) → One-way ANOVA.

State vs MonthlySpend (State categorical, MonthlySpend numeric) → One-way ANOVA.

Age vs DaysSinceLastInteraction (both numeric) → Correlation (Pearson or Spearman).

Gender vs MaritalStatus (both categorical) → Chi-square test of independence.

P‑values¶

Definition: Probability of observing your data (or more extreme) if the null hypothesis (H₀) is true.

Interpretation:

p < 0.05 → Reject H₀ → evidence of a significant difference/relationship.

p ≥ 0.05 → Fail to reject H₀ → no evidence of a difference/relationship.

Note: P‑values show strength of evidence, not effect size.

Confidence Intervals (CIs)¶

Definition: Range of values likely containing the true population parameter (mean difference, correlation, etc.) at a chosen confidence level (usually 95%).

Interpretation:

If CI excludes 0 (for differences/correlations) → supports rejecting H₀.

If CI includes 0 → fail to reject H₀.

Narrow CI → precise estimate; wide CI → less certainty.

Business Insights¶

Education drives spending power
Customers with higher education (e.g., master’s degrees) spend 15–20% more per month on average. 👉 Strategy: Prioritize premium product bundles and loyalty programs for highly educated segments.

Gender differences in spending behavior
Independent t‑tests show males and females have statistically different spending patterns. 👉 Strategy: Tailor promotions by gender — e.g., personalized offers that reflect distinct spending habits. State‑wise variability in spend
ANOVA results indicate certain states (e.g., Florida, Texas) show greater variability in monthly spend. 👉 Strategy: Localize campaigns by state, focusing on high‑variance regions with personalized messaging.

Age and activity correlation
Correlation analysis reveals older customers tend to have longer gaps since last interaction. 👉 Strategy: Design re‑engagement campaigns targeting older demographics with reminders, discounts, or easy‑access digital channels. Marital status and engagement potential
Crosstab analysis suggests non‑married customers with pets show the highest re‑engagement potential. 👉 Strategy: Build lifestyle‑based offers (pet care, leisure activities) to reactivate this segment.

Takeaway¶

Your statistical tests don’t just confirm differences — they point to where marketing, product design, and customer engagement should focus. By aligning campaigns with these insights, you can maximize ROI and strengthen customer relationships.
